# Literature appraisal of the Perturbase manuscript

This appraisal tests the manuscript's four load-bearing claims against retrieved primary literature: that human genetic evidence justifies target prioritisation, that knockdown-causality gating and artefact rejection are the right corrections, that the aggregate-DE ranking is a defensible readout, and that the TYK2 worked example is a real druggable win. For each it separates supportive from non-supportive evidence, then closes on what is genuinely new.

## Supportive evidence

### Genetic-evidence premise

The platform's central bet is that layering human genetic, clinical, and constraint evidence onto a functional screen improves target quality. This premise is among the better-supported claims in translational genomics. The foundational result is that drug mechanisms with human genetic support historically succeed through development at roughly twice the rate of those without ([Nelson 2015](https://doi.org/10.1038/ng.3314)), an estimate that survived independent re-analysis on a larger, cleaner dataset ([King 2019](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1008489)) and was refined again with a decade more attrition data, putting the relative success increase near 2.6-fold while showing the effect depends heavily on the class of genetic evidence and the therapy area ([Minikel 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07316-0)). The Open Targets Platform the manuscript consumes for its disease-association overlay operationalises exactly this idea ([Buniello 2024](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkae1128)). The caveat the manuscript underplays: [Minikel 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07316-0) is explicit that genetic support raises the *prior*, not the posterior. Most genetically supported programs still fail, and the enrichment is a population-level statistic, not a per-target guarantee. Perturbase's readiness engine treats genetic association as one capped facet among about twelve, which is the defensible design, but the TYK2 case should be read against this literature as a favourable prior rather than a confirmation.

### Knockdown gating and essential-gene rejection

The manuscript's most substantive methodological stance holds that downstream DE is uninterpretable unless the target's own knockdown is confirmed, and that near-zero-baseline or essential-gene "hits" are artefacts. Both parts are strongly concordant with the single-cell CRISPR-screen methods literature. The essential-gene confound is textbook: fitness genes deplete cells and distort screen signal, which is why the Cancer Dependency Map built dedicated corrections for gene-independent and copy-number effects ([Tsherniak 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2017.06.010)), and why later fitness-inference models such as Chronos exist specifically to separate true dependency from dropout artefact ([Dempster 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-021-02540-7)). Perturbase's diagnosis of 237 top candidates as essential-dropout artefacts (94% at gnomAD LOEUF below 0.35) is the same correction applied at the prioritisation layer, with constraint from [Karczewski 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2308-7) as the flag. The calibration concern is even more directly supported. The SCEPTRE line of work established that naive single-cell CRISPR-screen testing produces badly miscalibrated p-values and inflated false positives, and that conditional resampling is needed to restore calibration ([Barry 2021](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-021-02545-2)), a problem that intensifies at low multiplicity of infection ([Barry 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-024-03254-2)) and underpins genome-scale GWAS-locus screens ([Morris 2023](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.adh7699)). This is where the manuscript's honesty is well placed: it declines to reimplement SCEPTRE naively and degrades gracefully instead, a defensible choice given that a naive rewrite would reintroduce the miscalibration SCEPTRE was built to fix.

### TYK2 worked example

The TYK2 case is the manuscript's most concrete external anchor, and it holds up on retrieval. Deucravacitinib is a genuinely allosteric, pseudokinase-domain-binding TYK2 inhibitor, a mechanistically distinct class from active-site JAK inhibitors ([Wrobleski 2019](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jmedchem.9b00444)). It cleared two phase-3 psoriasis trials against both placebo and apremilast ([Armstrong 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jaad.2022.07.002); [Strober 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jaad.2022.08.061)) and showed phase-2 efficacy in systemic lupus ([Morand 2022](https://doi.org/10.1002/art.42391)). Because this is a marketed drug, a pipeline that surfaces TYK2 as a top hit with an autoimmune genetic association is recovering a validated target, a legitimate positive control. The honest framing, consistent with the genetic-evidence literature, is that TYK2 shows the pipeline recovers known biology, which is what a calibration positive should do. It is not independent evidence that the pipeline's novel hits will succeed.

### Primary-T-cell substrate

The manuscript's emphasis on primary human CD4⁺ T cells rather than a cell line is well grounded. The primary-T-cell functional-genomics literature it builds on shows consistently that context reshapes which regulators matter: genome-wide CRISPR screens revealing immune-function regulators ([Shifrut 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2018.10.024)), CRISPRa/i screens decoding stimulation responses ([Schmidt 2022](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abj4008)), Mediator-kinase and regulatory-gene perturbation architecture ([Freimer 2022](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abn5647)), and downstream target engineering such as RASA2 ablation ([Carnevale 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-05126-w)). This is direct support for the condition-resolved design, and it is also why the non-significant HIV host-factor cross-check (GSE318876, enrichment p = 0.535) is the correct result to report rather than bury. A T-cell-activation phenotype should not transfer cleanly to an HIV-dependency phenotype, and reporting that honestly is what separates a calibrated tool from an overclaiming one.

## Non-supportive evidence

### Contested DE-count readout

The ranking's most exposed claim is its readout. It consumes per-target DE-gene counts from the published aggregate table, and two independent lines of evidence cut against over-reliance on that statistic. First, single-cell DE without proper pseudobulk aggregation systematically inflates significance because cells from one donor are not independent replicates; treating them as such produces false discoveries that collapse when the analysis is done at the sample level ([Squair 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-25960-2)). The manuscript's upstream data is pseudobulked (DESeq2 on guide × donor × condition), which is the correct mitigation, but the DE-count readout still inherits the FDR-threshold sensitivity of the upstream calling. The manuscript's own honest finding that naive top-50-by-breadth overlaps strict-filtered top-50 in only 13 of 50 targets (Spearman r = 0.943) is the empirical signature of that fragility. Second, the field is moving toward causal-effect estimators that model the perturbation directly ([Dong 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-023-02040-5)) and toward predictive models of perturbation outcomes ([Roohani 2023](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41587-023-01905-6)), both of which treat raw DE-count ranking as a baseline to beat rather than an endpoint. Perturbase's ML tracks reportedly lost to simple baselines, consistent with the difficulty these papers document, which means the ranking's defensibility rests on the calibration result rather than on the DE-count statistic being intrinsically sound. The manuscript is right to anchor on the AUROC-0.85 benchmark; the literature says it must, because the underlying readout alone would not carry the claim.

### Prior versus guarantee

The genetic-evidence enrichment that motivates the whole overlay is a population-level prior, not a per-target posterior ([Minikel 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07316-0)). Most genetically supported targets still fail. Any language in the manuscript that reads as though a genetic association plus a screen hit constitutes validation runs ahead of this evidence and should be stated as a raised prior awaiting the one thing the computation cannot supply, prospective wet-lab confirmation.

## Novelty assessment

Being precise about novelty matters for a manuscript that could otherwise read as overclaiming. The screen itself is not this work's contribution; it is the published Zhu/Dann dataset, and Perturbase explicitly does not re-derive its regulatory networks. The DE method is not new, since it consumes DESeq2 pseudobulk output. The individual evidence sources are not new, as Open Targets, gnomAD, LINCS, STRING, Reactome, and AlphaFold are all established. The ML did not produce a new predictive method, and those tracks lost to baselines, which the manuscript reports honestly.

What is genuinely novel is narrower and defensible: the integration discipline. I could not find a prior tool that combines, at the per-target-card level, a four-state knockdown-causality gate that formally distinguishes *not-measurable* from *not-assessed* and refuses to penalise the latter; a hard red-flag override that caps essential, broad-effect, off-target, and batch-confounded targets regardless of downstream statistics; and a bidirectional control calibration reported with its unflattering numbers intact (only 20% of canonical positives clear strict grade ≥3). The `unknown ≠ 0` governance rule, applied consistently across sparse external overlays, is a genuine and uncommon design stance, because most scoring pipelines silently impute missing evidence to zero and thereby penalise exactly the novel, under-annotated targets a discovery effort exists to find. This is an engineering-and-epistemics contribution, not a biological or algorithmic discovery, and the manuscript is strongest when it claims exactly that.

## Bottom line

The premise (genetic evidence improves prioritisation), the corrections (knockdown gating, essential-gene rejection, calibration), and the worked example (TYK2) are all supported by verifiable primary literature. The most exposed claim is the DE-count ranking readout, which the single-cell methods literature treats as a fragile baseline; the manuscript's own 13/50 rank-instability finding is the tell, and its decision to anchor on the benchmark rather than the raw statistic is the right response. The honest non-supportive threads, that genetic support is a prior not a guarantee, that DE-count ranking is contested, and that cross-phenotype transfer failed, are already partially acknowledged and should be stated plainly rather than smoothed over. The novelty is integration discipline and calibrated honesty, not new biology or new algorithms; claimed at that level, it is defensible.
