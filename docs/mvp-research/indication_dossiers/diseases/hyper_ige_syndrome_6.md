# Hyper-IgE Syndrome 6, Autosomal Dominant, with Recurrent Infections

**Platform link**: Target gene **STAT3** (platform signed-DE rank 925, Open Targets genetic-association score 0.95).

## 1. Definition & Population
Autosomal dominant hyper-IgE syndrome (AD-HIES, also called Job syndrome or STAT3-HIES) is a rare inborn error of immunity caused by dominant-negative mutations in STAT3, presenting with recurrent skin and pulmonary infections, eczema, and markedly elevated serum IgE. It typically presents in infancy/childhood with characteristic facial, skeletal, and connective-tissue features alongside the immune phenotype.

## 2. Epidemiology Highlights
- No population-level prevalence/incidence figure was found in this pass; the disease is characterized as rare and has been studied mainly through case series and international cohorts assembled over decades. "Not established in this pass" for a specific prevalence number.
- The condition was first described clinically 55 years before a 2021 review, with STAT3 identified later as the causal gene, indicating a slow accumulation of a small global patient cohort rather than a large registry-documented population.

## 3. Biology Highlights
- STAT3-HIES arises from dominant-negative STAT3 mutations that confer "a broad immune defect across both innate and acquired arms, as well as defects in skeletal, connective tissue, and vascular function", producing the classic infection/eczema/skeletal triad.
- Pathophysiology research has "driven understanding of the interface of several signaling pathways, including the JAK-STAT pathways, interleukins 6 and 17, and the role of Th17 lymphocytes", directly implicating the JAK-STAT axis that STAT3 sits within — the same pathway family the platform's target-discovery approach is probing in CD4+ T cells.
- Phenocopies of the syndrome have been identified via mutations in other genes in the same pathway, including "identification of phenocopies such as mutations in IL6ST and ZNF341", reinforcing that upstream/downstream STAT3-pathway perturbation reproduces the phenotype.

## 4. Current Treatment
No FDA-approved disease-specific therapy targeting STAT3 was identified in this pass. Historically, treatment has centered on supportive and anti-infective care: "treatment has hinged on aggressive antimicrobial prophylaxis and treatment to prevent the development of end-organ disease such as pneumatocele", since early attempts at allogeneic stem cell transplantation were reported as not positive given STAT3's constitutive expression.
